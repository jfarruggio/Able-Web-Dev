8 Must Have Tools When Servicing High Efficiency Equipment.

A message from the Nordyne Technical Service Dept.: With the demands of todayâ€™s higher efficiency HVAC equipment, many of these models require more than basic hand tools for effective installation, and proficient troubleshooting should trouble arise. The NORDYNE Technical Service Dept. is staffed by TSAs that are experienced in the use of advanced testing instruments. To provide dealers and field technicians the most efficient service, we are requesting that when technicians are calling from the jobsite location of equipment that has experienced a malfunction, that they are also prepared with some of the most commonly used test instruments required to efficiently troubleshoot the problem. These test instruments include, but are not limited to:


  1. An electrical volt-ohm test meter
  2. An electrical amperage meter (The amperage function may be one already included into a multi-function electrical test meter). Gas furnace troubleshooting requires a meter that measures DC Micro amps.
  3. A refrigerant pressure manifold gauge set.
  4. A water tube manometer with gradients in inches of water column. A digital manometer is superior to a water tube, since it can be used when testing gas furnace pressure switch circuits.
  5. A thermometer or other form of temperature test probe. (Please note: Nordyne TSAs do not advise the use of laser type thermometers for most troubleshooting scenarios.)
  6. A digital combustion analyzer
  7. A dual-port magnehelic (This type of instrument is commonly implemented for testing forced air ducting static pressures.)
  8. A quality set of jumper test leads with alligator clips

Unfortunately, too often a field technician will have taken the time to contact the Technical Service Department, only to discover that he/she does not have access to the required test instruments needed to perform a thorough testing or troubleshooting procedure with a Nordyne TSA. This results in multiple call-backs, extended hold times, or hypothetical guess-work on the behalf of the TSA. This scenario may also result in unnecessary repair parts and/or warranty claims.

by Joey Farruggio / January 3, 2014
